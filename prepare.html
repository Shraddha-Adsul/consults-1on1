Getting the Most From Your Consultation

You've booked the time. Here's how to make it count.

A good consultation isn't just about what the doctor knows — it's about what you bring to the conversation. A little preparation goes a long way toward making sure you leave with the clarity you came for.

Before Your Session

Write down your top 3 questions.

Not ten. Three. This forces you to prioritize what actually matters most to you right now. If time allows, your doctor will address more — but knowing your top three ensures the most important things don't get lost.

Gather your recent records.

If you have recent lab results, imaging, prescriptions, or medical records, have them accessible. You don't need to organize them perfectly — just have them on hand in case they're relevant.

Think about your timeline.

When did this concern start? What have you already tried? What has your current provider told you? A brief chronology helps your doctor understand your situation quickly so you can spend more time on what to do next.

Know what you want to walk away with.

Are you looking for a diagnosis? A second opinion? A plan? Permission to worry less? Being clear about your desired outcome — even roughly — helps your doctor tailor the conversation to what you actually need.

During Your Session

Be honest.

This is a confidential, judgment-free space. The more candid you are about your symptoms, concerns, habits, and fears, the more useful the conversation will be.

Ask for plain language.

If something isn't clear, say so. A good doctor will never mind explaining things differently. That's what this time is for.

Take notes — or ask if you can record.

You'll retain more if you capture key takeaways in the moment. Most providers are comfortable with you recording the session for personal reference — just ask.

After Your Session

Review your notes within 24 hours.

While the conversation is fresh, write down the key takeaways, any follow-up steps, and questions that came up after the fact.

Share with your primary provider.

If the consultation produced recommendations or a new perspective, bring it to your regular doctor. This isn't about replacing your existing care — it's about augmenting it.

Come back when you need to.

These consultations aren't one-and-done. Health is an ongoing practice. When new questions come up, book another session.

These sessions are designed for guidance, education, and informed perspective. They are not a substitute for emergency care, ongoing primary care relationships, or formal diagnostic workups. If you're experiencing a medical emergency, please contact your local emergency services.
